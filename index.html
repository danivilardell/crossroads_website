Summary

This paper introduces Crossroads, a smart contract layer for chain-abstracted assets. In Crossroads, assets from nearly any chain are represented on a single backend blockchain as ERC-20 tokens. As a result, any asset can participate in smart-contract-based exchange, lending, or privacy applications on a single unified platform.

Crossroads relies on key encumbrance: a threshold signing committee holds encumbered keys controlling assets on each integrated chain, signing transactions only as authorized by smart contracts on the backend blockchain. Asset movements are fee-efficient, as ownership changes are recorded on the backend blockchain.

Crossroads enables permissionless, modular integration of new blockchains using pluggable oracles with flexible design options (zkBridge, TEE-based, hybrid). We implement a proof of concept across multiple public blockchains: Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Solana. We catalog a range of applications enabled by Crossroads, including universal wallets, cross-chain staking and lending, privacy-preserving payments, and private management of public blockchain assets.

The messy landscape of cross-chain interoperability

A bridge enables two blockchains to securely communicate between each other. However, to fully connect N chains via individual bridges, N² bridges are necessary, each one built, deployed, and audited separately. With the existence of over 150 active chains, this becomes heavily impractical. A hub-and-spoke design is a natural solution: connect each chain once to a central hub and every pair becomes reachable via that hub, with just N connections, far easier to audit than N² individual bridges.

But every hub in use today comes with strings attached. Integration is generally permissioned: operators decide which chains can be integrated, so only a handful of high liquidity chains ever do; the hub's logic is fixed at the protocol level (only enabling transfers or swaps); and it usually demands verification logic on each integrated chain, leaving chains without smart contracts, like Bitcoin, out. The ecosystem remains fragmented and to move between two chains that don't share a hub, assets need to hop through a chain of intermediary bridges and swaps, paying a fee at every step. Any application built on cross-chain interoperability inherits those fees, making them prohibitively expensive.

How these fees are estimated

Illustrative, on a $1,000 swap: we sum the standard published fees a typical aggregator route charges. Gas is extra, and real cost varies with size, liquidity, and routing — a well-optimized route on deep liquidity can be cheaper (a live LI.FI quote for ETH between L2s ran ~0.4% all-in), while thin or exotic pairs run higher.

  • Aggregator fee ≈ 0.25%, once — LI.FI's platform fee.
  • DEX swap ≈ 0.3% per hop — Uniswap's standard tier; ≈ 1% for thin long-tail pairs, plus slippage.
  • Bridge ≈ 0.05–0.25% per hop — Across, Stargate (0.06%), Axelar/Squid (0.1–0.25% for Cosmos corridors).
  • Bitcoin via THORChain ≈ 0.5% — 0.05–0.5% liquidity fee plus affiliate and outbound fees.

The cost and complexity of cross-chain interoperability is what Crossroads aims to eliminate.

How Crossroads works

Crossroads is a permissionless and programmable hub for blockchain assets. It represents assets from nearly any chain (even ones without smart contracts, like Bitcoin or XRPL) as ERC-20 tokens on a single programmable backend chain, where any contract can operate on them. It has three key properties that distinguish it from existing hubs:

Programmable

Any smart contract on the backend chain (AMMs, lending markets, staking contracts) automatically works atomically on assets from every integrated chain.

Permissionless

Any entity can integrate any chain to the system. Integrating a chain amounts to deploying its ERC-20 asset contract on the backend chain and an oracle relayingthe chain’s finalized transactions.

Minimal to integrate

Crossroads needs only a unidirectional oracle from the asset chain to the backend chain, any chain with eventual, verifiable finality can be integrated, with no protocol changes on the chain itself.

Our system consists of five components. Asset chains are the existing blockchains integrated into Crossroads where the assets are held. The backend chain is a programmable EVM chain where every asset that is deposited into Crossroads is minted and represented as an ERC-20 token. Asset contracts are the ERC-20 contracts that handle the minting, tracking, and burning of those tokens. Oracles inform the backend chain when a transaction has finalized on an asset chain. And a signing committee holds the keys to the deposit addresses, split into shares under a threshold scheme, so no one ever holds a whole key, and handle the signing of withdrawals from the system. In Crossroads, the committee is both a policy engine and not a custodian and signs a withdrawal only when the asset contract says you are allowed to make it. Via the use of threshold cryptography, no committee member ever holds the keys to the deposited funds. This policy based custody model is called key encumbrance. The keys controlling your assets on the native chain are generated and stored under a threshold signature scheme, and the policies for when and how they can be used to sign transactions are handled by the asset contract.
